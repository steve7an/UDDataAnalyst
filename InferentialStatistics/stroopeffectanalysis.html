Test a Perceptual Phenomenon - Stroop Effect by Steve T

Abstract

In psychology, the Stroop effect is a demonstration of interference in the reaction time of a task. When the name of a color (e.g., blue, green, or red) is printed in a color that is not denoted by the name (e.g., the word red printed in blue ink instead of red ink), naming the color of the word takes longer and is more prone to errors than when the color of the ink matches the name of the color. The effect is named after John Ridley Stroop, who first published the effect in English in 1935.

Dataset

In a Stroop task, participants are presented with a list of words, with each word displayed in a color of ink. The participant???s task is to say out loud the color of the ink in which the word is printed. The task has two conditions: a congruent words condition, and an incongruent words condition. In the congruent words condition, the words being displayed are color words whose names match the colors in which they are printed: for example RED, BLUE. In the incongruent words condition, the words displayed are color words whose names do not match the colors in which they are printed: for example PURPLE, ORANGE. In each case, we measure the time it takes to name the ink colors in equally-sized lists. Each participant will go through and record a time from each condition. Each row of the dataset contains the performance for one participant, with the first number their results on the congruent task and the second number their performance on the incongruent task. There are a total of 24 observation per category.

2. What is an appropriate set of hypotheses for this task? What kind of statistical test do you expect to perform?

\(\overline{u}_\Delta\) : The mean for the paired difference.

\(H_0:\overline{u}_\Delta = 0\) - The time taken for the participant to name the ink color for both congruent and incongruent condition should be the same. \(H_A:\overline{u}_\Delta \neq 0\) - The time taken for the participant to name the ink color for both congruent and incongruent condition is different.

As we do not know the population standard deviation of this test plus the sample size is smaller than 30, were looking at conducting a t-test here. So because were interested to see if the interference due to the incongruent word condition actually caused any difference in the response time for the participant, and the same participant is administered to both of the congruent and incongruent word conditions, this introduces a paired t-test because the two samples are related. Finally, since were checking for difference in the response time for the participant, the mean response time can indeed be smaller or larger thus this is a two tail t-test.

6. What do you think is responsible for the effects observed? Can you think of an alternative or similar task that would result in a similar effect? Some research about the problem will be helpful for thinking about these two questions!

Based on the experiment, J. Ridley Stroop developed two experimental theories to explain this occurence: The first theory was called - Speed of Processing Theory: He showed that the Interference occurs because, people are able to read words much quicker and to name the colour of the word is much more complex. The second theory was called - Selective Attention Theory: Interference occurs because; naming the actual colour of the words requires much more attention than reading the words. So in other words, were more adept at reading words than colors because were very used to doing that but unless our job or daily task revolves around naming colors, otherwise we would be slower in naming color versus just reading the name of the color. Thus in the situation of the incongruent condition, we would require more time to read the color and try to ignore the printed word. There are also other conflict tasks such as the Stroop effect, such as the Eriksen Flanker task, in which researchers try to determine the response time of the participants were a result of the proximity of the noise to the target or the similarity of the noise to the target. Basically the researchers wanted to know if the participants would get distracted by other elements in the search task if the other elements were closer to the target as compared to the other elements being very similar to the target. In another lesser known interference test, the Simon effect refers to the finding that reaction times are usually faster, and reactions are usually more accurate, when the stimulus occurs in the same relative location as the response, even if the stimulus location is irrelevant to the task. Simons original explanation for the effect was that there is an innate tendency to respond toward the source of stimulation. All three tasks demonstrated that the response time was shortest when the conditions are congruent.
